### Clean Version

```text
People prefer stability because it gives them a sense of security. Having a stable job usually means predictable income and lower risks. As a result, people feel less stressed and can plan their future more easily. For example, many people are more willing to take out a loan when they have a stable job and regular income.
```

### Writing Takeaway

The paragraph became usable once the task was reduced to:

1. one reason only
2. four short sentences
3. a final typo scan

The structure was not the main problem by the end. The remaining leak was spelling.

## Speaking Drill

### Prompt

`Describe a person you know who prefers a stable life.`

### First Answer Summary

The first answer had real content, but the response drifted because the background became too long before the point was made. Long sentences also triggered hesitation and grammar slips such as:

- `the first company, which I worked for`
- `his last work`
- `his skills was`
- unfinished filler `ĺ—Ż`

### Improved Answer

```text
I want to talk about my former supervisor. He is someone who really prefers a stable life. He stayed in the same company for many years, even though the salary was not very high and his skills were not very up to date. He was still afraid to change jobs because he wanted security and a predictable life. Personally, I think stability can make people feel safe, but sometimes it can also stop them from improving themselves.
```

### Speaking Takeaway

The answer became stable after switching to this pattern:

1. point to the topic in the first two sentences
2. give one clear fact
3. explain why
4. end with a short personal view

### Listening Takeaway

This set confirms a repeated Part 3 problem:

1. mentioned topic is being mistaken for answer
2. evaluation words are not being weighted heavily enough
3. first attractive option is sometimes locked in too early

The answer was usually confirmed by stance markers such as:

- `less obvious`
- `never occurred to me`
- `simple solution`
- `right to worry`
- `basic life skill`
- `shocked`
- `it's a shame`

## What Broke

- Typo control is still the main writing leak, even after the paragraph logic becomes clean.
- Speaking still becomes unstable when the answer starts with too much background.
- Long sentences increase both hesitation and grammar errors.
- In Listening Part 3, topic mentions are still being chosen before the attitude or evaluation is confirmed.

## Useful Rule Reminders

1. One body paragraph = one reason only.
2. Use the last 20-30 seconds of writing for typo scan.
3. In Part 2 speaking, point to the topic in the first two sentences.
4. If a speaking sentence starts to break, cut it into two short sentences instead of restarting.
5. In Listening Part 3, answer the question word, not the topic noun.
6. If the speaker says `not / isn't / won't`, eliminate the matching option immediately.
