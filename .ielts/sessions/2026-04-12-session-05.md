## Today's Bottleneck

The main listening problem was not basic comprehension. The real bottleneck was overload in computer-based Part 3 multiple choice:

- the stems felt too long
- the options felt too dense
- trying to decode everything before listening slowed reaction speed
- when overloaded, topic mentions were still being mistaken for confirmed answers

## Today's Objective

Test a lighter Part 3 method:

1. keep the question focus minimal
2. keep option labels short and contrastive
3. avoid full-set replay
4. use the set only to identify the real overload pattern

## Listening Takeaway

This set confirmed a new personal pattern:

1. when the stem is long, over-reading causes delay
2. when the options are dense, over-annotating can create a second layer of overload
3. the best current strategy is not detailed note-taking, but minimum reduction

## Working Method Confirmed Today

### Question focus

- keep the focus minimal and close to the stem
- do not force a big paraphrase if it slows you down
- examples:
  - `reason`
  - `wrong belief`
  - `surprised`
  - `why`

### Option labels

- keep them to the shortest contrast words possible
- the goal is not beauty, but fast discrimination
- examples:
  - `theatre pays`
  - `few buy now`
  - `more adverts`

### Annotation rule

- if the question is already clear, use mental compression only
- only type or mark a short label when the question feels overloaded
- annotation is a rescue tool, not a mandatory step

### Correction rule

- do not replay the whole set
- replay only wrong or unsure questions
- log: `question focus -> kill -> confirm -> saved rule`

## Saved Rules

1. Use the smallest question focus that still keeps the task clear.
2. Keep option labels short and contrastive; do not rewrite the whole option.
3. If the brain can already compress the question, do not add extra typed notes.
4. In compare questions, check which side really owns the feature.
5. In `why` questions, do not choose a topic mention if the complaint or confusion is somewhere else.

## Speaking Drill

### Prompt Set

- `Do you prefer a stable life or a changing life? Why?`
- `Do you like trying new activities? Why or why not?`
- `Do you enjoy learning things by yourself? Why?`

### What Broke

The main speaking problem was not lack of ideas. The real bottleneck was unstable delivery:

- answers sometimes started with background instead of a direct response
- too many ideas were packed into one sentence with repeated `and`
- translated phrases appeared under pressure
- once one phrase broke, the whole answer became less fluent

### High-Value Corrections

- `make more opportunities` -> `create more opportunities`
- `life quality` -> `quality of life`
- `liked try` -> `liked trying`
- `what should I learn` -> `what to study`
- `what steps should I follow` -> `what steps to follow`

### Stable Answer Patterns Confirmed Today

1. Answer directly in the first sentence.
2. Keep one sentence to one main job only.
3. Use one real example, then finish with a clear result.

### Useful Chunks Saved Today

- `change can create more opportunities for me`
- `my income increased nearly tenfold`
- `my quality of life improved a lot`
- `I have always liked trying new activities`
- `keep up with changes`
- `I usually prefer learning with a teacher`
- `what to study and what steps to follow`
- `That saved me a lot of time and helped me learn more efficiently.`

### Final Stable Versions

#### 1. Changing life

```text
I prefer a changing life because change can create more opportunities for me. In my twenties, my salary was only about 1,000 RMB per month, so I knew I had to make a big change. I started learning programming, and after that my income increased nearly tenfold. That experience greatly improved my quality of life.
```

#### 2. Trying new activities

```text
Yes, I have always liked trying new activities. I think this is important because it helps me update my skills and keep up with changes. As a result, I can adapt more easily to changes at work.
```

#### 3. Learning by yourself

```text
Not really. I usually prefer learning with a teacher when I want to learn something difficult. For example, when I wanted to become an Android engineer, I took a course. The teacher could tell me what to study and what steps to follow. That saved me a lot of time and helped me learn more efficiently.
```

### Speaking Takeaway

This practice block showed a clear improvement path:

1. direct answer first
2. shorter sentences
3. fixed natural chunks instead of translated phrases

Once the answer followed that pattern, fluency became much more stable.

## Reading Maintenance

### Set

Coasteering in North Devon, questions 8-14

### Raw Result

- Score: 3/6 attempted, then corrected to 6/6 after review
- Wrong: 8, 9, 11, 12
- Correct: 13, 14
- Extra note: 10 was left blank because `NOT GIVEN` had already been used twice and confidence dropped

### User's Real Error Notes

- 8: did not connect `animals in the sea` with `marine wildlife`
- 9: did not treat `transported` as a transport arrangement that contradicts `walk`
- 10: no matching phrase was found, then answer distribution anxiety led to leaving it blank
- 11: `more challenging` was incorrectly treated as the opposite of `most popular`
- 12: `£40` was misread as an extra fee instead of the main session fee

### What Broke

The reading problem was not passage locating. The real bottleneck was answer-type judgement:

- paraphrase matches were being missed
- strong words such as `guarantees`, `need to`, and `pay extra` were not being checked carefully enough
- `NOT GIVEN` and `FALSE` were still being confused
- answer distribution anxiety affected question 10

### Key Corrections

#### 8

- wrong choice: `NOT GIVEN`
- real contrast:
  - statement: `guarantees`
  - text: `on some days` / `may even be able to`
- answer: `FALSE`

#### 9

- wrong choice: `NOT GIVEN`
- real contrast:
  - statement: `need to walk`
  - text: `you're transported`
- answer: `FALSE`

#### 10

- problem: left blank
- text says you can get a drink or snack in the cafe
- text does not say this is included in the fee
- answer: `NOT GIVEN`

#### 11

- wrong choice: `FALSE`
- text says the thrill-seeker route is `more challenging`
- text says nothing about popularity
- answer: `NOT GIVEN`

#### 12

- wrong choice: `TRUE`
- real contrast:
  - statement: `pay extra`
  - text: `£40 per person, which covers the use of wetsuits, helmets and a buoyancy aid`
- answer: `FALSE`

### Reading Takeaway

This set confirmed three useful TFNG reminders:

1. `FALSE` means the text gives the opposite meaning, not just a missing phrase.
2. `NOT GIVEN` means the exact detail is missing, even if the topic appears nearby.
3. Never use answer distribution (`too many NG already`) to decide the next question.
